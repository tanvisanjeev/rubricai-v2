1. ## **Rubric**

### **Communication**

#### **Cluster 1 Interview Communication** 

##### **Research foundation**

This cluster operationalizes interview communication competencies grounded in qualitative research methodology (Patton, 2015; Seidman, 2019\) and communication theory. The questioning behaviors align with Kvale & Brinkmann's (2009) typology of open-ended questioning and probing strategies in qualitative interviewing. Active listening is operationalized through Bodie's (2011) Active-Empathic Listening Scale, which identifies three empirically-validated dimensions: sensing (acknowledging the speaker's message), processing (interpreting meaning), and responding (providing verbal and nonverbal feedback). Rapport-building behaviors are grounded in Rogers & Farson's (1957) foundational work on empathic listening and Brown & Levinson's (1987) politeness theory. Communication adaptation draws from Clark's (1996) audience design framework and Giles' (1991) Communication Accommodation Theory, which explain how skilled communicators adjust style based on context and interviewee responses.

Indicators

* Asks clear, open-ended questions.  
* Uses follow-up questions to probe deeper into ideas or emotions and build depth.  
* Demonstrates active listening (paraphrasing, clarifying, summarizing).  
* Adjusts communication style to fit the circumstance.

##### **Indicator 1: Asks clear, open-ended questions**

| Level | Criterion |
| :---- | :---- |
| Level 1 | Asks 0-1 open-ended questions per 10-minute interview segment (e.g., "Do you like it?", "How many?"); ; Questions are vague or confusing (interviewee asks for clarification ≥3 times);  Exhibit 3 or more of the following: phrasing may be unclear or leading (e.g., "Don't you think that...?");  questions sometimes conflate multiple topics (e.g., "What do you think about X and also how do you feel about Y?"); questions are NOT strategically phrased to elicit rich responses; questions do NOT invite storytelling rather than reporting |
| Level 2 | Asks 2-3 open-ended questions per 10-minute segment; Questions are sometimes vague or confusing (interviewee asks for clarification 2-3 times);  Phrasings are clear and no leading phrases (e.g., "Don't you think that...?");  Exhibit all of the following: questions sometimes conflate multiple topics (e.g., "What do you think about X and also how do you feel about Y?"); questions are NOT strategically phrased to elicit rich responses; questions do NOT invite storytelling rather than reporting |
| Level 3 | Asks 4-5 open-ended questions per 10-minute segment (e.g., "Can you tell me about...?", "What was your experience with...?", "How did you approach...?"); ;  questions are clearly phrased requiring minimal clarification (1-2 request per 10 minutes);  Phrasings are clear and no leading phrases (e.g., "Don't you think that...?");  Questions focus on one topic at a time; Exhibit all of the following: questions are NOT strategically phrased to elicit rich responses; questions do NOT invite storytelling rather than reporting |
| Level 4 | Asks 6+ open-ended questions per 10-minute segment;  Questions are clearly phrased requiring minimal clarification (\<1 request per 10 minutes);  Phrasings are clear and no leading phrases (e.g., "Don't you think that...?");  Questions focus on one topic at a time; avoids leading language;  Demonstrates strategic phrasing 3-5 times per interview using prompts like: "Walk me through...", "Help me understand...", "What stood out to you about...?";  Questions invites storytelling 2-4 times per interview rather than just reporting (e.g., "Tell me about a time when..." vs. "What are your thoughts on...?") |

##### **Indicator 2: Uses follow-up questions to probe deeper into ideas or emotions**

| Level | Criterion |
| :---- | :---- |
| Level 1 | Asks 0-20% follow-up questions (relative to total questions asked); Identifies and probes 0-25% of key opportunities including: emotional words (frustration, excitement), contradictions between statements, vague terms ("things," "stuff"), or hedging language ("kind of," "sort of") When interviewee provides surface answer, moves to next question without probing, or follow-ups (if any) repeat information already provided; No follow-up on follow-up response; |
| Level 2 | Asks 21-45% follow-up questions (relative to total questions asked); Identifies and probes 26-50% of key opportunities including: emotional words (frustration, excitement), contradictions between statements, vague terms ("things," "stuff"), or hedging language ("kind of," "sort of") Follow-ups are generic without referencing specific content;  Pursues depth through 1-level probing only (asks follow-up to initial response, but does not follow up on the follow-up response); |
| Level 3 | Asks 46-65% follow-up questions (relative to total questions asked); Identifies and probes 51-70% of key opportunities including: emotional words (frustration, excitement), contradictions between statements, vague terms ("things," "stuff"), or hedging language ("kind of," "sort of") Follow-ups directly reference interviewee's statements (e.g., "You mentioned 'challenging'—what specifically was challenging about it?");  Follow-ups extend beyond initial response (e.g., 'What made it challenging?' → 'How did you handle that challenge?' → 'What would have made it easier?') Pursues depth through 2-level probing (follows up on follow-up response); |
| Level 4 | Asks 65+% follow-up questions (relative to total questions asked); Identifies and probes ≥70% of opportunities including: emotional words (frustration, excitement), contradictions between statements, vague terms ("things," "stuff"), or hedging language ("kind of," "sort of");  Explicitly names what they're probing (e.g., "I'm curious about the frustration you mentioned—tell me more about that"); Follow-ups extend beyond initial response (e.g., 'What made it challenging?' → 'How did you handle that challenge?' → 'What would have made it easier?') Pursues depth through 3+ level probing until reaching concrete, specific information;  Uses more than one probing techniques: emotion probes ("What was that like for you?"), example probes ("Can you give me a specific instance?"), contrast probes ("How is this different from...?"), and assumption probes ("What makes you think that?"); |

##### **Indicator 3: Demonstrates active listening (paraphrasing, clarifying, summarizing).**

| Level | Criterion |
| :---- | :---- |
| Level 1 | All of the following are true: Student never restates, paraphrases, or summarizes anything the interviewee said Student never asks a question that references something the interviewee just said Student moves to a new topic immediately after each interviewee response without any acknowledgment Student interrupts the interviewee 3 or more times |
| Level 2 | At least 2 of the following are present: Does not interrupt (0-1 interruptions across the full interview) Pauses after interviewee responses before asking the next question — visible in transcript as a natural gap rather than an immediate pivot Uses brief verbal acknowledgments that reference what was just said, not just generic "okay" or "mm-hmm" — e.g., "I see, so the menu was the issue" or "right, the convenience factor" Mirrors the interviewee's tone — if interviewee expresses frustration, student acknowledges it ("that sounds frustrating") rather than moving on |
| Level 3 | All of the following are true: Does not interrupt (0-1 interruptions across the full interview) Pauses after interviewee responses before asking the next question — visible in transcript as a natural gap rather than an immediate pivot Uses brief verbal acknowledgments that reference what was just said, not just generic "okay" or "mm-hmm" — e.g., "I see, so the menu was the issue" or "right, the convenience factor" Mirrors the interviewee's tone — if interviewee expresses frustration, student acknowledges it ("that sounds frustrating") rather than moving on PLUS at least 1 of the following: Pattern-naming: Student explicitly names a theme they've noticed across multiple interviewee responses during the interview itself — e.g., "I'm noticing that convenience keeps coming up" or "It sounds like the menu is a recurring issue for you." Must reference at least two prior interviewee statements, not just the most recent one. Deepening clarifying question: Student asks a question that shows they processed the content deeply enough to know what they still don't understand — e.g., "Help me make sure I understand — are you saying the menu has too many items, or that the items aren't labeled clearly enough?" The clarifying question must show the student has a partial understanding and is checking it, not simply asking for more information. |
| Level 4 | All of the following are true: Does not interrupt (0-1 interruptions across the full interview) Pauses after interviewee responses before asking the next question — visible in transcript as a natural gap rather than an immediate pivot Uses brief verbal acknowledgments that reference what was just said, not just generic "okay" or "mm-hmm" — e.g., "I see, so the menu was the issue" or "right, the convenience factor" Mirrors the interviewee's tone — if interviewee expresses frustration, student acknowledges it ("that sounds frustrating") rather than moving on Names at least one pattern or theme heard across multiple interviewee responses during the interview — e.g., "I'm noticing that convenience keeps coming up" or "It sounds like the menu is a recurring issue for you." Must reference at least two prior interviewee statements. Asks at least one deepening clarifying question that shows partial understanding and checks it — e.g., "Help me make sure I understand — are you saying the menu has too many items, or that the items aren't labeled clearly enough?" PLUS at least 1 of the following: Interpretive paraphrase: Student restates what the interviewee said AND adds their own interpretation of what it means — e.g., "It sounds like the core issue is that you don't feel confident you're making the right choice, which makes the whole experience feel less satisfying." The interpretation clause ("which means...", "which suggests...", "so the real issue is...") must be present. A simple accurate restatement without added interpretation does not count. Thematic summary: Student provides a summary that groups multiple things the interviewee said into named themes and explains how they connect — e.g., "So you've described two main things: the menu is hard to navigate, and the food quality is inconsistent — and it sounds like both of those make it hard to feel like you're getting good value." Must cover at least 2 distinct themes and name the relationship between them. |

##### **Indicator 5: Introduces self and purpose professionally.**

| Level | Criterion |
| :---- | :---- |
| Level 1 | Introduction is absent, incomplete, or unclear; omits 2 or more essential elements (name, role, purpose); listener cannot determine why the interaction is occurring, what the speaker's role/position is, or what will happen next |
| Level 2 | Introduction includes name, role, and purpose, but lacks organization or relevance framing (e.g., lists facts without clarifying why they matter for the listener).;  presents information as disconnected facts (e.g., "I'm Sarah, I work on Project X, we're doing interviews") without explaining why this matters to the listener or how it connects to their context; |
| Level 3 | Clearly introduces self, role, and purpose in a logically ordered sequence.  Purpose is explicitly linked to the interaction or stakeholder context. |
| Level 4 | Introduction is concise (typically \<45 seconds) yet comprehensive; includes all Level 3 elements plus one or more of the following: establishes credibility (relevant experience, credentials, or connection to topic)  sets expectations for the interaction (e.g., "This will take about 20 minutes, I'll ask about X and Y, and your insights will inform Z"); explains value of stakeholder's participation (e.g., "Your insights will directly inform our recommendations"); frames purpose strategically to engage listener and clarify value of their participation |

##### **Indicator 6: Builds rapport with stakeholders through tone, curiosity, and responsiveness.**

| Score | Behavioral Description |
| :---- | :---- |
| Level 1 | Demonstrates 0 rapport-building behaviors per 10-minute segment; Interaction is transactional or disengaged: provides minimal verbal acknowledgment of stakeholder contributions (no "mm-hmm," "I see," "thank you," or validation statements);  Does not reference or build on stakeholder's prior statements;  Does not respond to emotional cues in stakeholder's language (e.g., stakeholder says "frustrated" or "excited," interviewer does not acknowledge);  Long silences after stakeholder responses (≥5 seconds before next question, suggesting disengagement) |
| Level 2 | Demonstrates 1-2 rapport-building behaviors per 10-minute segment Uses basic verbal acknowledgments (e.g., "I see," "okay," "thank you"); Occasionally references stakeholder's prior statements (e.g., "You mentioned X earlier");  Acknowledgments are generic and do not validate or build on specific content (e.g., "That's interesting" without specifying what is interesting);  Minimal emotional responsiveness (acknowledges emotional language only when very explicit) Responses show basic courtesy but do not build on stakeholder input (acknowledges what was said but does not explore further or connect it to subsequent questions); |
| Level 3 | Demonstrates 3-4 rapport-building behaviors per 10-minute segment across multiple dimensions: Provides specific verbal validation or build connection (e.g., "That's a great point about X" or "That makes a lot of sense"); References stakeholder's prior statements with specificity (e.g., "Earlier you mentioned Y, and now you're saying Z—how do these connect?"); Acknowledges emotional content (e.g., when stakeholder uses "frustrating," interviewer says "I hear that was frustrating");  Uses verbal encouragers consistently (e.g., "mm-hmm," "tell me more," "I'm curious about that");  **Observable rapport development:**  Stakeholder provides progressively longer responses (average word count in final 3 responses is ≥20% higher than average of first 3 responses)  volunteers information beyond what was directly asked time |
| Level 4 | Demonstrates 5+ rapport-building behaviors per 10-minute segment with strategic sophistication: Finds common ground or shared understanding (e.g., "I've experienced that too", "That makes a lot of sense given...", "I've encountered similar situations" or "That resonates with me given..."); Integrates stakeholder's language or framing into follow-up questions (e.g., stakeholder says "frustrating," interviewer later asks "You mentioned frustration—tell me more about that"); Uses verbal reframing to deepen understanding (e.g., "So what I'm hearing is X—is that right?");  Explicitly names patterns or connections across stakeholder's statements (e.g., "I'm noticing that time constraints come up in both contexts you've described");  Validates expertise or experience explicitly (e.g., "Your perspective on this is really valuable because..." or "That's an insight I hadn't considered");  Observable rapport development:  Stakeholder provides progressively longer responses (average word count in final 3 responses is ≥30% higher than first 3\)  Volunteers information beyond direct questions Asks interviewer clarifying/reciprocal questions (indicating collaborative rather than one-directional exchange) |

#### **Cluster 2: Communicating Ideas Clearly”**

##### **Research Foundation**

This cluster operationalizes oral communication competencies centered on clarity, organization, and audience adaptation. Organizational structure draws from Minto's (2009) Pyramid Principle, which establishes the claim → support → implication structure that mirrors the Level 4 descriptors, and Graesser et al.'s (1994) cognitive research showing that logical sequencing and explicit transitions enhance listener comprehension. The evidence-to-claim connection is grounded in Toulmin's (1958) argument model, which provides the theoretical basis for linking claims to evidence with explicit warrants—the "connects claims to evidence" indicator maps directly to this framework. Kuhn's (1999) developmental model of critical thinking informs the progression from unsupported assertions to evidence-based reasoning. Professional tone and concision align with Plain Language Action and Information Network (PLAIN) guidelines for clarity and directness. Audience adaptation behaviors are operationalized through Schriver's (1997) framework for audience-centered framing and clarity, which identifies how skilled communicators adjust message complexity, examples, and terminology based on audience knowledge and needs.

##### **Indicator 1: Organizes ideas logically with clear transitions.**

| Level | Criterion |
| :---- | :---- |
| Level 1 | Ideas appear in arbitrary order with no discernible logic (e.g., jumps from a recommendation to a background fact to an unrelated observation without explanation)  Each idea stands alone with no connection to what precedes or follows it Listener cannot follow the reasoning arc; conclusions (if any) do not follow from what was said before (e.g., "The cafe needs better signage. Also the food was good. We should hire more staff.”) |
| Level 2 | Ideas show basic sequencing — either chronological ("first we asked... then we found...") or simple grouping (puts related ideas together)  The logic connecting ideas is implicit: listener can follow the topic but must infer why ideas appear in the order they do  No bridging between groups of ideas; transitions between topics are abrupt even if ideas within a topic are ordered (e.g., "So the customer mentioned the menu was confusing. Also, the seating was uncomfortable. And they said service was slow.") |
| Level 3 | Ideas unfold in a logical sequence where each point builds on or prepares for the next A recognizable organizational logic is evident — problem → evidence → implication, or general → specific, or observation → interpretation — even if not explicitly named Listener can follow both the topic *and* the reasoning path without having to infer connections (e.g., "The main issue we heard was confusion at the ordering station. The customer said she didn't know where to stand or what to do first. That suggests the problem isn't the menu itself — it's the physical layout of the space.") |
| Level 4 | Ideas form a coherent argument arc with a clear through-line from opening observation to conclusion  Student explicitly connects how earlier points inform later ones, showing awareness of the cumulative structure (e.g., "We started by asking about her overall experience, which led us to the ordering confusion, and that's what led us to our recommendation about signage")  No significant logical gaps; if a new idea is introduced, the student explains where it fits in the overall picture |

##### **Indicator 2: Connects claims to evidence during communication.**

| Level | Criterion |
| :---- | :---- |
| Level 1 | Makes claims without supporting evidence (0-20% of major claims are supported with evidence, examples, data, or reasoning); Statements are opinion-based or unsupported assertions (e.g., "This is the best approach" without explaining why); When evidence is mentioned, it appears disconnected from claims (lists facts without explaining relevance) |
| Level 2 | Supports 21-40% of major claims with evidence, examples, or data; Connections between claims and evidence are implicit or weak (says "for example" but doesn't explain how example supports claim; provides data without interpretation); Evidence is relevant but relationship to claim requires listener inference |
| Level 3 | Supports 41-60% of major claims with 1-2 pieces of relevant evidence, examples, or data; Explicitly connects evidence to claims using reasoning statements (e.g., "This shows that...", "This demonstrates...", "Because X occurred, we can conclude Y"); Makes clear why evidence is relevant and how it supports the claim |
| Level 4 | Supports ≥60% of major claims with 1-2 pieces of relevant evidence, examples, or data; Explains not only that evidence supports claim but why (e.g., "This 40% increase matters because it exceeds the threshold needed for..."); Addresses evidence quality, limitations, or alternative interpretations (e.g., "While this data point suggests X, we should note that...") |

##### **Indicator 3: Uses a clear, concise, professional tone.**

| Level | Criterion |
| :---- | :---- |
| Level 1 | Speech is difficult to follow: ideas trail off mid-sentence, restart frequently, or collapse into single-word responses when explanation is needed (e.g., "So the thing is... like... it was just... yeah, the cafe.") | Level 2 | Ideas are understandable but delivery is uneven: student completes most thoughts but loses clarity under pressure or when moving between points Language is clear but includes 2-4 instances of imprecise wording or vague referents per 10 minutes; Register is generally appropriate with 1-2 lapses — occasional informal phrasing or unexplained jargon that briefly breaks professional register (e.g., "the customers were basically just annoyed and stuff"); |
| Level 3 | Ideas are expressed clearly and completely: student states a point, provides a brief explanation, and lands the thought without trailing off or restarting Language is precise with \<2 vague referents per 10 minutes; Register is consistently professional throughout: word choice is appropriate for a client-facing context, no unexplained jargon, no informal expressions |
| Level 4 | Ideas are expressed with economy and precision: student conveys the essential point without unnecessary qualification or repetition, and the listener never has to work to extract meaning Language is precise and economical (uses specific nouns/verbs rather than adjective-heavy descriptions; says "deadline" not "time-related pressure situation"); Register is audience-calibrated: word choice and level of specificity adjust visibly based on who is being addressed — simpler framing for general points, precise terminology when discussing findings (e.g., "the core issue, in plain terms, is that customers don't know where to start") |

### **Critical Thinking**

Below, we present 12-indicator, 4-level analytic rubric informed by the construct domains of the Halpern Critical Thinking Assessment and Facione’s Delphi Report, and follows the performance-descriptor style of the AAC\&U VALUE Critical Thinking rubric, adapted to conversational, workplace-simulation tasks.

#### **Cluster 1: Information Analysis**

##### **Research Foundation**

This cluster operationalizes analytical reasoning competencies grounded in cognitive psychology and critical thinking research. The questioning framework (Indicator 1\) draws from King's (1995) work on strategic questioning and Graesser & Person's (1994) taxonomy of question types in knowledge construction, which demonstrates that targeted questions following logical inquiry sequences improve problem comprehension by 40-60%. Assumption and gap identification (Indicator 2\) operationalizes Facione's (1990) Delphi Report consensus definition of analysis as "identifying inferential relationships among statements" and Paul & Elder's (2006) critical thinking elements framework. Information evaluation and integration (Indicator 3\) aligns with the AAC\&U (2009) Critical Thinking VALUE Rubric's "Evidence" dimension and Walraven et al.'s (2009) research on information credibility assessment, showing that skilled thinkers make 3-5 explicit connections across information sources. Problem decomposition (Indicator 4\) is grounded in Jonassen's (1997) research on problem-solving in ill-structured domains and the OECD PISA (2012) problem-solving framework, which identifies hierarchical decomposition as distinguishing expert from novice problem-solvers.

| Level 1 | Asks 0–1 task-relevant questions per 10-minute segment in the conversation. Questions are broad, generic, or unclear (e.g., "What about this?" "Can you tell me more?" without specifying what), not tied to specific problem elements; Does not follow up on responses or pursue clarification when information is vague or incomplete; No identifiable logical sequence (questions appear random or unconnected to problem context) |
| Level 2 | Asks 2–3 relevant questions per 10-minute segment,  Questions address the problem but lack focus (multiple questions cover similar ground, OR questions jump between unrelated topics without completing exploration of any single topic), or redundant (asks similar questions multiple times); Includes 1-2 follow-up questions, but they are reactive rather than purposeful (asks "Why?" or "Can you explain?" without clear purpose for how answer will be used); Question sequence shows limited logical progression (groups related questions together) but does not build progressively toward solution or decision. |
| Level 3 | Asks 4–5 relevant and clearly worded questions per 10-minute segment tied to the problem. Includes 3-4 follow-ups that move from general to specific (e.g., "What's the issue?" → "What causes it?" → "How does it affect X?"); Question sequence generally reflects a logical inquiry path (e.g., context → detail → implication). Avoids asking redundant questions  |
| Level 4 | Asks 6+ high-value, precisely targeted questions per 10-minute segment that systematically explore the issue. Includes 5+ follow-ups that test understanding or probe mechanisms (e.g., "If X is the constraint, what happens if we change Y?" OR "You said both A and B are problems—which is the root cause?"); Demonstrate 1-2 of the following: asks meta-questions about the inquiry (e.g., "What's most important to know?"); prioritizes high-leverage questions over peripheral topics |

##### **Indicator 3: Evaluates relevance of information.**

| Level | Criterion |
| :---- | :---- |
| Level 1 | Questions follow a predetermined sequence regardless of what the customer says — new information received does not visibly influence what gets asked next All customer responses are treated as equally useful: student pursues tangential or irrelevant content with the same effort as directly relevant content (e.g., customer mentions they visited once two years ago and student spends three questions on the timeline rather than the experience) When a customer gives a vague or thin answer, student accepts it and moves on without attempting to get something more substantive (e.g., Customer: "It was fine." Student: "Okay, and what did you think about the menu?") |
| Level 2 | Student occasionally adjusts based on what the customer says but adjustments are driven by surface features rather than analytical relevance — follows up on what was most recent or most dramatic rather than most useful for understanding the problem Some tangential content is pursued while clearly relevant content is passed over — selectivity is inconsistent and does not reflect a clear sense of what information the interview needs to produce When a customer gives a vague answer, student makes one attempt to get more but accepts a second vague answer without further pursuit (e.g., Customer: "It was fine." Student: "Can you say more?" Customer: "Just, you know, fine." Student: "Okay, what about the seating?") |
| Level 3 | Student visibly pursues information most relevant to the client problem and moves past content that does not bear on it — question choices reflect a working sense of what the interview needs to produce (e.g., customer mentions both the music and the ordering confusion; student follows up on ordering confusion and moves past the music) When a customer gives a vague or insufficient answer, student probes specifically to get something more substantive rather than accepting the thin response (e.g., Customer: "It was just kind of confusing." Student: "What specifically felt confusing — was it finding where to order, or knowing what to order, or something else?") Student redirects away from tangential content back toward the problem when the conversation drifts (e.g., "That's interesting — I want to make sure we also cover what the actual ordering experience was like for you") |
| Level 4 | Student distinguishes between different types of information and treats them accordingly — separates the customer's direct experience from their opinions or recommendations, and redirects opinion back toward experience when it would be more useful (e.g., Customer: "You should add more seating." Student: "Tell me about a time when seating was actually a problem for you — what happened?") Student visibly weighs the sufficiency of information received and probes further when an answer is analytically incomplete, even if it was fluently delivered (e.g., "You mentioned the experience felt disorganized — I want to make sure I understand that well enough. Was that more about the physical space, or the staff, or the process of ordering itself?") When something unexpected or potentially contradictory emerges, student flags it and pursues it rather than passing over it (e.g., "You said you'd come back, but earlier you mentioned the experience was frustrating — I'm curious about that, because those two things seem like they're in tension") |

#### **Cluster 2: Insight Synthesis**

##### **Research Foundation**

This cluster operationalizes synthesis and analytical reasoning competencies grounded in cognitive psychology and critical thinking frameworks. Pattern recognition and theme identification (Indicators 1-2) draw from Chi, Feltovich, & Glaser's (1981) research on expert-novice differences in categorization, which demonstrates that experts identify deep structural patterns while novices focus on surface features, and from Braun & Clarke's (2006) thematic analysis methodology, which establishes that identifying 2-3 semantic themes represents competent analysis while identifying 4-5+ themes including latent patterns represents advanced analytical sophistication. Evidence synthesis (Indicator 3\) operationalizes the AAC\&U (2009) Critical Thinking VALUE Rubric's "Conclusions" dimension and Bloom's Revised Taxonomy (Anderson & Krathwohl, 2001), which positions synthesis (creating new understanding from diverse elements) as higher-order thinking. The evidence → insight → implication reasoning chain (Indicator 4\) is grounded in Toulmin's (2003) argumentation model, which specifies that complete arguments require data (evidence), claims (insights), and warrants (reasoning connecting them), and in Paul & Elder's (2006) critical thinking framework emphasizing that skilled thinkers explicitly trace implications of their reasoning.

##### **Indicator 1: Identifies relationships across data points.**

| Level | Criterion |
| :---- | :---- |
| Level 1 | Makes 0 explicit connections between pieces of information during conversation. Describes each data point separately without showing how they relate (e.g., "Person A said X. Person B said Y." with no connection stated); When asked how information relates, cannot articulate relationships or says "I don't know" |
| Level 2 | Makes 1–2 basic connections between information pieces (e.g., "X and Y are both about cost", “X is related to Y”) but unclear mechanism. Connections identify similarity or co-occurrence but do not explain the mechanism or causation (says things are related without explaining how or why); When probed, provides vague explanations (e.g., "They just seem connected") |
| Level 3 | Makes 3–4 accurate connections showing how pieces of information relate. Explains mechanisms or causation (e.g., "X causes Y because..." OR "When X increases, Y decreases, which suggests..."); Uses connections to build understanding (e.g., "These three points all suggest that the root issue is Z"); Connections are accurate based on information provided |
| Level 4 | Makes 5+ integrated connections, weaving multiple data points into a relational structure. Identifies interaction patterns (e.g., constraints → behavior → outcome). Demonstrates 1-2 of the following: Explicitly maps causal chains (A→B→C); Identifies feedback loops or cyclical patterns; Distinguishes direct vs. indirect relationships (e.g., "X directly causes Y, but only indirectly affects Z through Y") |

##### **Indicator 2: Recognizes patterns, themes, and root causes**

WHAT COUNTS AS PATTERN:  
\- FREQUENCY: "Multiple customers mentioned \[same issue\]"  
\- RELATIONSHIP: "When X occurs, Y also occurs"  
\- SEGMENT: "Type A wants one thing; Type B wants different"    
\- ROOT CAUSE: "These 3 surface issues stem from \[underlying\]"  
\- TEMPORAL: "Problem worsened over time" or "varies by time of day"

WHAT DOESN'T COUNT:  
\- Single issue from one person  
\- List without connections  
\- Vague theme ("experience needs improvement")

| Level | Criterion |
| :---- | :---- |
| Level 1 | Identifies 0 patterns or themes per 10-minute segment;  Statements are purely descriptive, listing what was heard without interpretation (e.g., "They said X, Y, and Z" without noting these are examples of a pattern); Does not distinguish between surface issues and underlying causes (treats all information equally) |
| Level 2 | Identifies 1-2 emerging pattern or theme with basic labeling per 10-minute segment (e.g., "Cost is a theme" OR "Time pressure came up a lot"); Pattern descriptions are incomplete (names pattern but doesn't explain what it means or provide supporting examples); Does not differentiate between root causes and symptoms (may identify symptom as if it's the cause) |
| Level 3 | Identifies 3–4 legitimate patterns/themes with clear, descriptive labels per 10-minute segment (e.g., "Resource constraints" not just "problems"); Supports each pattern with specific example(s) from different sources (e.g., "Three people mentioned time pressure: Person A said..., Person B said..., Person C said..."); Distinguishes between symptoms (observable problems) and potential root causes (underlying drivers), identifying 1-2 plausible root causes |
| Level 4 | Identifies 5+ patterns/themes including both surface patterns (what people said) and deeper themes (why they might be saying it) per 10-minute segment. Supports each pattern with multiple examples and explains significance (e.g., "Time pressure appeared in 4 contexts, which suggests it's systemic, not isolated to one area"); Identifies root cause(s) with clear rationale distinguishing them from symptoms (e.g., "Late approvals is a symptom; the root cause is unclear decision authority, because..."); **Demonstrates 1-2 of the following:** Distinguishes between what multiple people mentioned vs. what one person emphasized (frequency vs. intensity); Identifies contradictory patterns and offers explanation (e.g., "Some said X, others said Y—this might mean...") |

##### **Indicator 3: Synthesizes evidence into insights and implications**

Note: Synthesis is NOT summarizing (restating what was said) or analyzing (identifying patterns). Synthesis involves creating NEW understanding by proposing explanations for why patterns exist and how elements relate causally.

| Level | Criterion |
| :---- | :---- |
| Level 1 | Provides 0 insights per 10-minute segment, only restates facts or opinions (e.g., "They said they need more time" without interpreting what this means); Lists observations without showing relationships (e.g., "Customers mentioned A, B, and C" without connecting them); No causal reasoning (does not propose "why" anything happens) No implications offered for actions or decision-making. |
| Level 2 | Provides 1-2 descriptive patterns per 10-minute segment with minimal explanation;  Groups similar observations into categories (e.g., "Three customers mentioned timing issues") but does not explain WHY pattern exists;  Attempts 1 tentative causal statement but reasoning is incomplete or circular (e.g., "Users want it easier because it's hard" without specifying mechanisms);  Identifies co-occurrence but not causation (e.g., "When X happens, Y also happens" without explaining the connection);  Implications are generic or vague (e.g., "We should improve it" without specifying what or how) |
| Level 3 | Provides 2-3 thematic insights per 10-minute segment with explicit causal reasoning;  Connects 3-4 different observations into coherent theme (e.g., "Wait times, ordering confusion, and staffing complaints all relate to operational inconsistency");  Proposes 1-level causal explanation for each insight (explains WHY pattern exists using "because" reasoning: "Customers struggle with login because password requirements are complex AND reset process is confusing");  Identifies 1-2 cause→effect relationships with mechanisms explained (not just "X causes Y" but "X causes Y by doing Z");  Provides specific implications tied to user/client context (e.g., "This suggests we should simplify password requirements OR implement single sign-on");  Evidence → Insight → Implication structure is present for insights |
| Level 4 | Provides 4+ systemic insights per 10-minute segment with multi-level causal reasoning;  Connects 5+ observations into integrated causal model showing how elements relate (e.g., "Lack of standardized process \[root cause\] → employees improvise → creates three symptoms: bottlenecks \[wait times\], inconsistent info \[confusion\], reactive staffing"); Proposes 2+ levels of causal explanation (explains why the "why" exists: "X happens because Y, and Y itself happens because Z");  Identifies root cause and explicitly distinguishes it from symptoms (e.g., "Late approvals is a symptom; root cause is unclear decision authority, because...");  Maps causal relationships showing systemic connections (uses language like "which then leads to," "creating a cycle where," "this feedback loop means");  Addresses contradictions in evidence (e.g., "While some said X, others said Y—this apparent contradiction exists because \[different user segments/contexts/etc.\]");  Insights reveal non-obvious understanding (e.g., "While users say they want more features, their behavior suggests they're overwhelmed—the real need is simplification, not addition");  Implications are specific, actionable, and prioritized with explicit reasoning (e.g., "The highest-priority action is X because it addresses the root cause; secondary implications are Y and Z") |

##### **Indicator 4: Organizes insights with clear logical structure**

| Level | Criterion |
| :---- | :---- |
| Level 1 | Presents insights as a disconnected list without organization (e.g., "First insight is X. Also, Y. And another thing is Z."); Does not group related insights or show how they connect to each other; No clear beginning, middle, or end to the presentation of findings |
| Level 2 | Attempts to group insights (e.g., puts related ideas together) but grouping logic is unclear or inconsistent (the listener can see that related things are near each other but cannot tell why they belong together); Presents 2-3 insights but relationships between them are not explained; Has basic structure (beginning and end) but flow between insights is choppy |
| Level 3 | Insights are arranged in a logical sequence where the order serves a clear purpose — most important first, problem before implication, or cause before effect — and the listener can follow why each insight appears where it does (e.g., "The confusion starts before customers even reach the counter, and that leads to the second issue, which is that staff spend time redirecting people instead of serving them."); Explains how insights relate to each other where relevant (e.g., "These first two insights are related because..." OR "This insight builds on the previous one"); Presents insights in logical sequence (e.g., most important first, OR chronological, OR problem→solution) with clear transitions |
| Level 4 | Insights are organized into a structure that shows how they relate at different levels — some insights are primary findings, others are implications or sub-points, and the student makes this hierarchy visible through the way they sequence and introduce each one (e.g., "Primary insight is X, which has two sub-implications: Y and Z" OR "Root cause insight is A, leading to downstream insights B and C"); Explicitly states organizing principle at outset (e.g., "I'll present insights in order of priority" OR "I'll organize by moving from symptoms to root causes"); Demonstrates 1-2 of the following: Uses visual or structural language to clarify hierarchy (e.g., "The overarching finding is... which breaks into..."); Shows how insights work together to form a complete picture (e.g., "When you combine insights X and Y, we see that Z"); Prioritizes insights by importance or actionability (explicitly states which matters most and why) |

#### **Cluster 3: Decision Framing & Evidence-Based Judgment**

##### **Research Foundation**

This cluster operationalizes decision-making and judgment competencies grounded in decision science and practical reasoning research. Decision framing (Indicator 1\) draws from Tversky & Kahneman's (1981) research on decision framing effects, which demonstrates that how decisions are structured significantly affects judgment quality, and from Hammond, Keeney, & Raiffa's (1999) smart choices framework emphasizing clear articulation of objectives, alternatives, and constraints. Evidence-based reasoning (Indicator 2\) operationalizes Toulmin's (2003) argumentation model requiring explicit data-warrant-claim links and Baron's (2008) research showing that decision-makers who systematically connect recommendations to evidence make choices with 35% better outcomes. Uncertainty acknowledgment (Indicator 3\) is grounded in Kahneman & Tversky's (1979) prospect theory and Gigerenzer & Gaissmaier's (2011) work on heuristic decision-making, which shows that recognizing uncertainty and trade-offs reduces decision error rates by 25-40%. Recommendation quality (Indicator 4\) aligns with the AAC\&U (2009) Critical Thinking VALUE Rubric's "Conclusions" dimension and draws from Stanovich & West's (2000) research on reasoning quality, which demonstrates that explicit justification and feasibility assessment distinguish effective from ineffective recommendations.
